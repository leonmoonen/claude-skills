# SFF-VI Phase 2 Assessment Criteria — Full Text and Review Guidance

Verbatim criteria text from the Phase 2 call, with "Phase 2 only" sub-points marked as in the original
(these did not apply in Phase 1 and are new ground for the applicant to cover). Each criterion is followed
by review guidance oriented around the fact that SFF-VI is a **comparative ranking competition**, not a
threshold gate — the question for every section is not just "is this good" but "would this stand out
against ~30 other centre-scale proposals a committee reads back to back."

Use this as the rubric for the SKILL.md review workflow's criterion-by-criterion step.

---

## 1. Excellence – potential for advancing the state-of-the-art

> The extent to which
> - the proposed research addresses important research challenges that will have great impact on
>   international research themes and/or research methods.
> - the objectives are ambitious and beyond the state-of-the-art (e.g. novel concepts and approaches,
>   development of novel methodology or development between or across disciplines).
> - the proposed research has the potential to achieve ground-breaking results.

**What reviewers are really asking**: is this a genuinely field-shaping bet, and is it the kind of bet that
specifically needs a 10-year, multi-group *centre* to pursue — as opposed to something a single FRIPRO-scale
project could tackle? This last point is easy to overlook: a proposal that reads like "a great single-PI
research programme, just bigger" is vulnerable, because the whole premise of SFF is that the centre
structure itself unlocks something a smaller unit couldn't.

Weaknesses to look for:

- The "why does this need 10 years and 3–6 groups" case isn't made explicitly — ambition described in
  isolation from the organisational form needed to pursue it.
- Cross-disciplinary framing that's asserted ("this brings together X and Y") without a concrete account of
  what the combination will actually produce that neither discipline could alone.
- The proposal restates the Phase 1 pitch without deepening it — since Phase 2 gives 3x the space, a draft
  that reads like Phase 1 padded out with detail, rather than substantively developed, wastes the
  opportunity Phase 2 exists to provide.
- No explicit positioning against the leading international groups/centres working on adjacent problems —
  reviewers assessing "great impact on international research themes" need a sense of the competitive
  landscape, not just the applicant's own logic.

## 2. Excellence – quality of R&D activities

> The extent to which
> - the outlined scientific approach is feasible.
> - the proposed theoretical approach, scientific methods and research design are appropriate to address
>   the scope and complexity of the research questions and to achieve the scientific goals of the centre.
>
> Phase 2 only: The extent to which appropriate consideration has been given to ethical issues, safety
> issues and gender dimensions in the research content.

**What reviewers are really asking**: given the scale and complexity being proposed, is the actual research
design credible — not just "would this be nice to have" but "will this specific, large, multi-group
machine actually produce the claimed results."

Weaknesses to look for:

- Feasibility claims pitched at the level of the centre's mission ("this centre will transform our
  understanding of X") without a corresponding account of how the sub-projects/work packages/research
  themes concretely combine to get there.
- Research design that reads as a loose federation of each group leader's existing individual research
  agenda, rather than a genuinely coordinated design — a common and serious risk for multi-PI centres.
  Reviewers are specifically told to weigh "the extent to which the proposal goes beyond what the individual
  group leaders could achieve alone" (see Implementation), so this criterion and that one should be checked
  together: if the science here doesn't require the group leaders to work together, the centre case weakens
  on both counts.
- Ethics/safety/gender-dimension treatment that is generic boilerplate rather than tailored to the specific
  hazards or dimensions of *this* research (this is new in Phase 2 — a Phase-1-style proposal that never
  addressed this at all needs a genuinely new section, not a token paragraph).
- No discussion of how methodological or theoretical risk will be identified and managed over a decade —
  10-year horizons make early risk-hedging especially important; a plan that reads as fixed and unchanging
  for 10 years is a credibility problem given how much a field can shift in that time.

## 3. Impact

> The extent to which the centre will contribute to the development of an internationally leading research
> environment in Norway.
>
> Phase 2 only:
> - The extent to which the centre will create an environment that will attract and train research talents
>   beyond what could be attained in the individual research groups.
> - The quality and scope of communication and engagement activities with different target audiences.

**Important framing**: this criterion is explicitly about Norway's research environment and human capital,
not (primarily) about societal/policy impact in the way some other RCN schemes score it. A proposal that
reads Impact purely as "our societal relevance is X" without addressing environment-building and talent
development is answering a different question than the one being asked.

Weaknesses to look for:

- No concrete account of how the centre will out-perform the sum of its parts at attracting/training talent
  — e.g. joint PhD training across groups, shared seminars/schools, career pathways for postdocs, specific
  plans to recruit internationally. Vague claims ("we will attract top talent") without mechanism are a
  common, fixable gap.
- Dissemination/communication plans that are copy-pasted boilerplate rather than scoped to the actual
  target audiences relevant to this centre's research (policy communities, industry, the public,
  practitioners — whichever are genuinely relevant).
- International partner funding (if any) not justified in terms of how it strengthens the *Norwegian*
  environment specifically — RCN explicitly limits funding to foreign partners and wants this justified.
- No discussion of what happens to the research environment after RCN funding ends (post-centre
  sustainability) — since a centre is meant to leave behind a strengthened, leading environment, not just
  spend 10 years of grant money, a credible legacy/continuation angle strengthens this criterion materially.

## 4. Implementation

> Centre director — the extent to which
> - the centre director has demonstrated the ability to conduct ground-breaking research.
> - the centre director has the required scientific expertise and capacity to successfully lead the centre.
> - the centre director has demonstrated sound leadership in the training and advancement of young
>   scientists.
>
> Group leaders, for which CVs are submitted — the extent to which
> - the group leaders have demonstrated the ability to conduct ground-breaking research.
> - the group leaders have the required scientific expertise and capacity to successfully execute the
>   project.
> - at least one of the group leaders, in addition to the centre director, has a track record of
>   significant research achievements.
>
> Centre organisation
> - The extent to which the proposal goes beyond what the individual group leaders could achieve alone.
>
> Phase 2 only: the extent to which
> - the group successfully demonstrates in the proposal that it brings together the elements — such as
>   skills, knowledge, experience, expertise, disciplines, methods, approaches — necessary to address the
>   proposed research question.
> - the management structures and governance are appropriate.
> - the physical organisation of the centre is optimal for scientific collaboration and an inspiring
>   student environment for supervision and collaboration across the research themes.
>
> Phase 2 only: if the group consisting of the centre director and the group leaders demonstrates gender
> imbalance, the extent to which there are adequate plans in place to support the development of research
> talents from the under-represented gender, with the goal of qualifying them for senior-level positions.

**What reviewers are really asking**: can *this specific team*, with *this specific organisational design*,
actually run a 10-year centre — leadership capability, complementary expertise across the group leaders,
and workable governance, all at once.

Weaknesses to look for:

- Centre director's leadership/training track record described only via publication metrics rather than
  concrete evidence of mentoring, team-building, or having run something at scale before (a lab, a large
  grant, a network) — "demonstrated sound leadership in training and advancement of young scientists" is an
  explicit, separate sub-criterion from raw scientific excellence, and easy to under-write.
- The "at least one other group leader with a track record of significant research achievements" requirement
  is explicit in the text — check this is unambiguously true and clearly evidenced for at least one named
  group leader, not just implied by seniority.
- Governance structures described abstractly ("a steering committee will oversee the centre") without
  naming who's on it, how decisions get made, how conflicts between group leaders are resolved, or how the
  Scientific Advisory Committee will be composed and used.
- Physical/organisational plan for co-location, shared space, and cross-theme student interaction is thin
  or assumed — this is now an explicit Phase 2 sub-criterion ("physical organisation... optimal for
  scientific collaboration and an inspiring student environment"), so a floor plan or description of shared
  space/seminars is worth more here than it would be in a typical grant proposal.
- If the leadership team (director + group leaders) is visibly gender-imbalanced and the proposal has no
  plan addressing it, this is a direct, checkable, explicitly-scored gap in Phase 2 — flag it plainly.
- Complementarity of group leaders asserted rather than shown — check each group leader's stated expertise
  maps onto a distinct, named piece of the centre's research design, with no obvious redundant overlap or
  unexplained gap (e.g. a core planned method with no group leader who has done it before).
