## What SFF is, and what makes Phase 2 different from Phase 1

Norwegian Centres of Excellence (SFF) fund **10-year centres**, not projects — the scheme exists to let
Norway's strongest research environments organise into a centre with long-term basic funding, pursuing
ambitious, complex objectives that need sustained, coordinated effort (often but not always
interdisciplinary). This is categorically a bigger bet than a FRIPRO researcher project: bigger budget,
longer horizon, a whole leadership team and organisation being assessed, not just one PI.

SFF-VI is a **two-phase** process:

- **Phase 1** (closed, 12 Nov 2025 deadline): open to all applicants, short project description (5 pages),
  reviewed by three subject-matter subcommittees (Humanities & Social Sciences / Life Sciences / Natural
  Sciences & Technology) who selected **36 applications, evenly split at 12 per subcommittee/domain**, to
  advance to Phase 2.
- **Phase 2** (this call, deadline 11 Nov 2026): reserved for the applicants invited forward from Phase 1.
  Must describe **the same project** as Phase 1 — RCN will not read the Phase 1 application unless there's
  doubt it's the same project, in which case the Phase 1 text may be pulled for comparison. This means
  Phase 2 is fundamentally an *elaboration*, not a rewrite: the core scientific bet, centre director, and
  named group leaders must all match Phase 1 (see "What cannot change" below).

**The single biggest thing that distinguishes SFF from FRIPRO, and should shape how you read a draft**:
SFF-VI is decided by **comparative ranking against the other Phase-2 applicants**, not by clearing an
absolute qualification threshold. The referee panel for each application only ever reviews *that one
application* (it cannot compare across applications) and produces marks plus a preliminary assessment.
Then a **Scientific Committee** for Phase 2 reads all applications, the panel assessments, and the
applicant's rebuttal, and **ranks all Phase 2 applications against each other**, proposing a final list of
**roughly 12** of the 36 Phase 2 applicants for funding (36 applications advanced from Phase 1, split evenly
at 12 per subcommittee/domain: Humanities & Social Sciences, Life Sciences, Natural Sciences & Technology).
The call text describes a mechanism with some guaranteed top slots reserved per domain and the remainder
filled by merit regardless of domain — check the live call page for the precise current split, since exact
figures have shifted (an earlier version of this document assumed 15 funded centres; the actual number is
closer to 12). A moderate gender tiebreak (favoring a woman centre director) applies only among applications
assessed as otherwise equal. This means the review lens should always be relative: not just "is this good"
but "is this distinguishable and memorable against the ~35 other centre-scale proposals (roughly 12 per
domain) a committee is ranking side by side" — with only about 1 in 3 of the Phase 2 pool ultimately funded.

## Rebuttal step — a real, usable opportunity

After the referee panel's preliminary assessment and marks are produced, **the applicant receives them and
can respond** ("rebuttal") — but only to clarify scientific misunderstandings in the panel's assessment,
not to re-argue disagreements about judgment or priorities. The panel can choose whether to incorporate the
rebuttal into its final assessment. Two practical implications for reviewing a draft: (1) a proposal that
pre-empts likely misreadings in the text itself needs no rebuttal and looks stronger from the start; (2) it
is worth flagging to the applicant, while reviewing, any place a generalist reviewer could plausibly
misunderstand a claim — that's exactly the kind of gap the rebuttal mechanism exists to fix, but relying on
the rebuttal is a weaker position than getting it right the first time.

## Who's eligible / what cannot change between Phase 1 and Phase 2

- Only an application invited forward from Phase 1 may apply to Phase 2 — this is not an open call.
- The **centre director must be the same person(s)** named in Phase 1 (if Phase 1 had two directors taking
  turns, both must still be named for the same periods).
- The **group leaders/PIs for whom CVs are submitted must be the same individuals as in Phase 1** — you
  cannot swap out, add, or drop named group leaders between phases, and cannot add new CVs in Phase 2.
  (Once a centre is actually funded and running, PI changes are possible with governance approval — but
  not during the Phase 1 → Phase 2 transition itself.)
- Centre director eligibility: doctoral degree or associate-professor-level qualification before the
  deadline (institute-sector *forsker 1/2* or *seniorforsker* counts as equivalent); must be employed by
  the host institution at project start; can direct only one Phase 2 application; cannot be centre director
  of an SFF-V centre still receiving RCN funding in 2028; can appear on other SFF-VI applications only in a
  non-director role.
- Host institution ("Project Owner") must be an RCN-approved Norwegian research organisation, and must
  commit to co-locating the centre, providing suitable premises, contributing administrative services, and
  having internal guidelines supporting SFF centres.

## Funding scale and duration

- **NOK 80–160 million total over 10 years** from RCN — this is a centre-level envelope, not a per-PI
  budget; it must be read against the ambition and size of the group being assembled.
- Contract term is 10 years, with an **underway assessment roughly at the halfway point (~year 5)** that
  can lead to revised conditions for the remaining years. Frame long-term plans (dissemination, succession,
  post-funding continuation) with this checkpoint in mind — reviewers of the *proposal* won't score the
  underway assessment, but a credible discussion of how the centre will demonstrate progress at that
  checkpoint (interim milestones, not just decade-out ambitions) signals a well-thought-through Implementation
  case.
- Planned start: between 1 Jan 2028 and 1 Aug 2028; latest completion date 31 Jul 2038.
- **Basic funding vs. supplementary funding**: basic funding (RCN's SFF grant + secured co-financing from
  host/partners) is the only funding entered directly in the application form (first 96 months/8 years only
  — the form has no room for all 10); the full 10-year basic *and* supplementary funding picture goes in the
  mandatory "10-year budget" Excel attachment. Supplementary funding is anticipated/estimated income (e.g.
  future competitive grants, EU projects) that supports the centre's goals — it's a credibility signal, not
  a guarantee, and its realism should be checked against the centre's track record of winning such funding.
- **Own financing is not formally required**, but RCN explicitly recommends it and notes that historically
  host institutions have contributed roughly as much in own-financing as RCN's own grant — a proposal with
  little or no visible institutional co-investment is worth flagging as a signal of weaker institutional
  backing, even though it's not a hard rule.
- Maximum 60% of RCN's total 10-year SFF contribution can be budgeted in the first 72 months (pre-underway-
  assessment period) — check the funding plan respects this if the budget attachment is available.
- No cap on equipment purchases, but note: the *host institution*, not the centre, owns purchased equipment;
  the centre pays a usage/rental charge, and if a shared facility ("leiested") is used near its full
  capacity for years, the centre may be charged close to its full cost.
- RCN does not fund state aid — funding must go to the non-economic (research) activity of host/partners.

## Attachments and page limits (Phase 2)

- **Project description**: 1 front page + max 15 pages + 1 page reserved for references only (17 pages
  total, single attachment). This is 3x the Phase 1 length (5 pages) — Phase 2 is where the applicant has
  room to actually substantiate the claims that Phase 1 only sketched.
  - Front page: fixed-format info (title, subject area, project summary ~2000 characters, primary
    objectives ~1000 characters in the templates seen at Phase 1 — exact character counts should be
    confirmed against the current template, but note RCN enforces the font-size/format rules on this page
    strictly to preserve system readability; don't let an applicant try to cheat font size down to fit more).
  - Footnotes are allowed but discouraged except for abbreviation explanations (min. 9pt, within margins).
  - No animations in figures; no active hyperlinks anywhere (links are excluded from assessment).
  - References must be complete (article title + full author list; >5 authors may use "et al. (N authors
    total)"; centre director/group leaders who are co-authors may be bolded and their author position noted).
- **Centre director CV + track record**: CV (max 2 pages) + 10-year track record (max 2 pages) = 4 pages,
  one attachment per director.
- **Group leader/PI CV + track record**: same 2+2 page structure, for a **minimum of 2 and maximum of 6**
  group leaders (max 5 if there are two centre directors, so max 7 CVs total including director(s)). Younger
  group leaders who'd qualify for FRIPRO's Early Career Scientists call (2–7 years post-PhD) may use the
  "early achievements" track record template instead of the 10-year one — advise using whichever template
  makes that person's case strongest, not a rigid default.
- **10-year budget** (mandatory Excel attachment): full 10-year basic + supplementary funding picture.
- **Optional**: up to 3 additional suggested international referees (beyond any suggested at Phase 1).
- Templates are mandatory and strictly enforced: content that doesn't fit the template, or that exceeds
  page limits, **will not be assessed at all** — this is a harder rule than "weakens the application", it's
  "invisible to reviewers." Anything load-bearing must be inside the compliant template pages.

## Assessment process, step by step

1. Formal compliance check by RCN administration (reject if requirements unmet, if project owner/partner
   had a serious breach of RCN obligations in the past 2 years, or if the project manager was found guilty
   of research misconduct in the past 2 years).
2. **Referee panel** (minimum 3 non-Norway-based referees) reviews the single application in isolation,
   each submits an individual assessment, then the panel meets and produces a **consensus panel assessment
   and marks on all four criteria** (dissent noted if any panel member disagrees).
3. **Rebuttal**: applicant receives the preliminary panel assessment/marks and may respond to scientific
   misunderstandings only.
4. **Scientific Committee for Phase 2** (10 members: 3 humanities/social sciences, 3 life sciences, 3
   natural sciences/technology, 1 interdisciplinary chair; none based in Norway; distinct membership from
   Phase 1's committee) reads all applications + panel assessments + rebuttals, **interviews the proposed
   centre directors**, and produces the ranked list of applications recommended for funding (roughly 12 of
   the 36 Phase 2 applicants).
5. Portfolio Board for Ground-breaking Research approves the process and makes the final funding decision.
   Results expected autumn 2027.
6. Feedback to applicants: final referee panel assessment + a brief Scientific Committee statement.

The **interview stage** is a distinctive, high-stakes step with no FRIPRO equivalent — the centre director
should be preparing to defend the proposal's core bets verbally to a committee of leaders from the applicant's
broad domain (not narrow specialists), not just in writing.

## Requirements and guidelines document — organisational commitments worth checking in the proposal

The Norwegian-language "Krav og retningslinjer" document sets out what a funded centre must actually do —
useful for spotting proposals that promise things inconsistent with these binding norms, or that omit
governance structures RCN will require anyway:

- **Governance**: the host institution must appoint a **centre board** (with at least one representative
  per partner, if any); the board approves accounts/annual report, is informed of strategies for securing
  supplementary funding, and decides whether a new project counts as centre-supporting supplementary funding.
- **Scientific Advisory Committee (SAC)**: every centre must have one, ideally with international top
  researchers, to advise on scientific strategy and challenges throughout the centre's life. SAC members
  must not themselves be involved in the centre's research (if they become involved, they must leave the
  SAC) — a proposal naming SAC members who are also active collaborators on the centre's own research has
  a real conflict-of-interest problem worth flagging.
- **Co-location**: centres should consist of co-located research groups at the host institution; the host
  must provide suitable shared premises within a year of contract signature. A group leader based mainly
  elsewhere (partner institution or abroad) is expected to be physically present at the host institution a
  significant part of the time.
- **International mobility**: centres are expected to facilitate PhD/postdoc research stays abroad and to
  host visiting international researchers (including adjunct/professor-II appointments) — a proposal with
  no mobility plan at all is missing something RCN explicitly wants to see funded.
- **Gender balance**: if the leadership group (director + group leaders) is gender-imbalanced, the centre
  must have a concrete plan to develop/qualify under-represented-gender researchers toward senior positions
  — and RCN specifically scores this under Implementation in Phase 2 when imbalance exists. Absence of such
  a plan when the leadership team is visibly imbalanced is a concrete, checkable gap.
- **PhD training**: centres are expected to contribute high-quality PhD training and cross-group exposure
  for students/postdocs beyond what a single research group could offer — this connects directly to the
  Impact criterion's "environment that attracts and trains research talent beyond individual research
  groups."
- **Acknowledgement requirement**: all resulting publications must credit "Research Council of Norway" +
  "Centres of Excellence" + project number — not a review point, but worth mentioning if a dissemination
  plan section is being drafted.

## Practical drafting notes worth flagging (from RCN's own Q&A)

- Non-researcher specialists (e.g. clinicians, engineers) can be named in the text to support feasibility
  even without a CV attached — useful if the team includes essential expertise beyond the CV-eligible group
  leaders.
- The committee explicitly wants the Implementation-relevant parts of a group leader/director's expertise
  *surfaced in the project description text*, not left implicit in the CV — a proposal that assumes the
  CV speaks for itself is leaving an easy win on the table.
- Templates should be followed exactly, including tables/headings, even in the CV — the review committees
  have said they value applications looking uniform for easy comparison.
- A centre proposing to fund one or more international partners financially must justify in the text how
  that specifically strengthens the Norwegian research environment (this maps directly onto the Impact
  criterion, which is about developing a leading research environment *in Norway*).
