# SFF-VI Drafting Pitfalls — Distilled from RCN's Q&A

Source: RCN's Norwegian-language Q&A for SFF-VI (https://www.forskningsradet.no/finansiering/hva/sff/sporsmal-svar-sff-vi/),
published for Phase 1 but mostly still relevant to Phase 2 mechanics (CV/track record templates, partner
definitions, budget structure, and process rules carry over between phases; only the project description
template and length are Phase-2-specific and longer). Use this as a checklist of concrete, easy-to-miss
compliance and drafting details when reviewing a draft — these are the kinds of small things that don't
show up in the assessment criteria text but that RCN's own answers flag as recurring applicant confusions.

## Formatting rules that are actually enforced

- Footnotes are discouraged beyond abbreviation explanations; minimum 9pt font, must stay within margins.
- No active hyperlinks anywhere in the application or attachments — they are excluded from assessment, so
  nothing load-bearing should be "linked out" rather than written in.
- No animations in figures.
- The project description's front page has fixed, required fields (title, subject area/domain choice,
  project summary, primary objectives) with enforced character limits and font size — RCN has explicitly
  refused requests to shrink font size to fit more text. Don't let a draft try to game this.
- References must be complete: article title + full author list (not just journal name); if >5 authors, use
  "First author et al. (N authors total)" and may bold/number the centre director's or group leaders'
  author position for their own publications.
- Margins are strict — only a header with centre name/host institution and page numbers is allowed there.

## CV and track record template

- The template is a fixed table format and should be kept as-is, including boxes/fields that don't apply
  (leave them empty rather than deleting them) — reviewers have said they value every CV looking uniform so
  they can find the same information in the same place across all applications. A CV that's been
  restructured to look different from the template is a minor but real ding.
- Guidance/instructional text within the template can be shortened to save space, but keep an explanatory
  heading (e.g. "Representative publications") rather than deleting the section entirely.
- Career breaks (parental leave, etc.) can be described, and the "10-year" window for the track record can
  be extended to account for such breaks — a reviewer should not assume a career gap reflects unproductive
  time without checking whether this was addressed.
- Non-first-author publications can be included, but the applicant's specific contribution should be
  described — the committee is likely to weight these less than first/corresponding-author work, so an
  applicant leaning heavily on co-authored papers without explaining their role is a real, checkable weakness.
- A younger group leader who separately qualifies for FRIPRO's Early Career Scientists call (2–7 years post-PhD)
  may use the shorter "early achievements" track record template instead of the full 10-year one — recommend
  whichever version makes that specific person's case look strongest, not a default choice.
- Clinical/non-research specialist training and experience should generally go under "Positions" (with a
  note that the role didn't include research time), not under "career breaks" (which is meant for things
  like parental leave).

## Common, avoidable process/compliance mistakes

- Group leaders/PIs and the centre director must be employed by the host institution *or* a formal
  collaborating partner — if a named group leader's institution isn't already a partner, either the
  institution needs to become a formal partner (with an institutional collaboration agreement) or the
  person needs a part-time (≥20%) appointment at the host institution before contract signature. A draft
  that names a group leader from an uninvolved institution, with neither arrangement in place, has a real
  eligibility gap to flag.
- "Collaborating partner" means an *organisation*, not an individual — don't confuse a valued individual
  collaborator with a formal partner requirement; only list organisations RCN and the applicant are willing
  to sign an institutional-level agreement with, since that agreement is legally required before contract
  signature and is described as administratively demanding. Over-listing partners casually is a practical
  risk worth flagging.
- Departments/faculties within the *same* host institution are never "collaborating partners" — only
  external organisations count.
- Letters of support from people/organisations that aren't official partners or aren't otherwise requested
  (e.g. Scientific Advisory Committee members, informal collaborators) will simply be stripped from the
  application before review — recommend describing such contributions in the project description text
  instead of attaching unsolicited support letters.
- If a foreign group leader's institution isn't a formal partner (because no CV was submitted for them, or
  because they'll take a host-institution appointment instead), the confirmation-letter and 20%/4-week
  requirements don't apply to that person — but this only works if no CV is being submitted for them; if a
  CV *is* submitted, the requirements bind.
- Fellowship budgeting: the application form only has room for ~20 named fellowship line items, which is
  often too few for a centre-scale budget. RCN's suggested workaround is to skip individual fellowship
  entries and instead state planned totals in the funding-plan specification text (e.g. "Planned number of
  fellowships for the full centre period: doctoral 15, postdoctoral 15, overseas grants 15, visiting
  researcher grants 15"). A draft that appears to have tried to force every fellowship into the limited
  form fields (or that provides no aggregate planning figures at all) is worth a comment.
- No work-package-level budgeting is required for SFF (unlike some other RCN schemes) — don't penalize a
  draft for not having one, though the budget should still visibly track to the described research themes
  and organisational structure at a coarser level.
- Equipment/infrastructure cost rules follow RCN's general "leiested" (shared facility) framework — the host
  institution owns equipment; the centre pays a usage charge proportional to actual use. A budget that
  includes large equipment purchases without addressing who owns/operates them and how usage is apportioned
  is a gap worth a comment, though there's no fixed cap on equipment spending.
- The Impact criterion must be answered within the project description itself, not only in the application
  form's free-text boxes — the review panel/committee does not see the web form's own guidance text or
  necessarily its every field, so anything scored under the assessment criteria needs to live in the
  project description.

## Framing observations worth passing on

- Career-development/talent-pipeline framing (mentoring, early-career opportunities) is explicitly *not*
  scored in Phase 1 but *is* scored in Phase 2 (Impact: "attract and train research talents"; Implementation:
  director's leadership in "training and advancement of young scientists"). A Phase 2 draft that still reads
  like a pure excellence-of-the-PI pitch, without this dimension, is under-using space Phase 2 specifically
  opened up.
- Interdisciplinary applications can request assessment by two subcommittees/domains and should make an
  explicit case for which domain is "primary" — worth checking a genuinely interdisciplinary Phase 2 draft
  still clearly signals which of the three subject areas (Humanities & Social Sciences / Life Sciences /
  Natural Sciences & Technology) it's competing within, since that determines which domain's guaranteed
  funding slots (out of the ~12 centres funded across all three domains) it's competing for.
- Both interdisciplinary and single-discipline research can score well on Excellence — interdisciplinarity
  is not scored as inherently better, so a draft shouldn't force an interdisciplinary framing where the
  actual science doesn't need it just because it sounds more ambitious.
