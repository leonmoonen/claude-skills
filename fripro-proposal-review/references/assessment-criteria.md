# FRIPRO Assessment Criteria — Full Text and Review Guidance

This is the exact criteria text from the call (verbatim sub-bullets preserved), each followed by guidance
on what tends to separate a 5 (not funded) from a 6–7 (funded) on that criterion. Use this as the rubric
for Step 3 (Criterion-by-Criterion Assessment) in SKILL.md.

Remember the qualification rule: **all four criteria must independently score 6 or 7**. Treat each section
below as a pass/fail gate, not a point to average against the others.

---

## 1. Excellence – potential for advancing the state-of-the-art

> The extent to which the proposed work is ambitious, novel, and goes beyond the state-of-the-art
> - Scientific creativity and originality.
> - Novelty and boldness of hypotheses or research questions.
> - Potential for development of new knowledge beyond the current state-of-the-art, including significant
>   theoretical, methodological, experimental or empirical advancement.

**What reviewers are really asking**: if this works, would it change what the field knows or how it works?
Is this an incremental extension dressed up as a breakthrough, or a genuine bet on something new?

Weaknesses to look for when reviewing a draft:

- The proposal reads as "more/better/faster" of an existing paradigm rather than a genuinely new question.
  Ask: could a strong PhD student have proposed this as a natural next step? If so, the novelty case is
  probably too thin for this call.
- The state-of-the-art section merely lists prior work without showing the gap the project fills, or
  without acknowledging the strongest existing counter-approaches.
- Hypotheses are vague or unfalsifiable ("we will explore how X relates to Y") rather than sharp, risky
  claims that could be wrong.
- No explicit acknowledgment that the work is risky/bold — FRIPRO explicitly wants risk, and a proposal
  that reads as safe and low-risk undersells itself even if it is scientifically solid. Check that the
  proposal names its own boldest bet rather than hiding it.

What to recommend: a crisp 2–3 sentence articulation of "the current ceiling is X; we propose Y, which
would move that ceiling because Z" early in the proposal (ideally in the summary), plus explicit contrast
with the closest competing approaches or research groups.

## 2. Excellence – quality of R&D activities

> The quality of the proposed R&D activities
> - Quality of the research questions, hypotheses and project objectives, and the extent to which they are
>   clearly and adequately specified.
> - Credibility and appropriateness of the theoretical approach, research design and use of scientific
>   methods. Appropriate consideration of interdisciplinary approaches.
> - The extent to which appropriate consideration has been given to ethical issues and gender dimension in
>   research content, and the use of stakeholder/user knowledge if appropriate.

**What reviewers are really asking**: even if the idea is exciting, will the proposed methods actually
answer the question, and does the applicant clearly know how to execute it?

Weaknesses to look for:

- Research questions/objectives that don't map cleanly onto the methods section — e.g. an objective about
  causal mechanism paired with a purely correlational/observational design, with no discussion of the gap.
- Methods described at a level too abstract to assess feasibility ("we will use machine learning to analyze
  the data") instead of concrete choices (which models/techniques, why, what data, what validation).
- No discussion of alternative approaches considered and why the chosen one is credible — reviewers want to
  see that the design wasn't the first idea that came to mind.
- Ethics and gender-dimension considerations bolted on as boilerplate rather than tailored to the actual
  research content (e.g. a genuinely generic GDPR paragraph when the project involves sensitive human data
  and should discuss consent, re-identification risk, etc. specifically).
- No risk/contingency plan: FRIPRO explicitly says it is willing to fund high-risk work as long as the
  applicant shows how risk will be managed and what the fallback is if the main approach doesn't pan out.
  A proposal with no "if X doesn't work, we will do Y instead" is a common, avoidable gap.

What to recommend: a short risk table (main risks × likelihood × mitigation/fallback) if not already
present, and a check that every stated objective has a correspondingly concrete method.

## 3. Impact

> Potential impact of the proposed research
> - Potential for academic impact: the extent to which the planned outputs of the project address important
>   present and/or future scientific challenges; the extent to which the planned outputs are openly
>   accessible to ensure reusability of the research outputs and enhance reproducibility.
> - Potential for societal impact (if addressed by the applicant): the extent to which the planned outputs
>   of the project address UN Sustainable Development Goals or other important present and/or future
>   societal challenges.
> - The extent to which the potential impacts are clearly formulated and plausible.
>
> Communication and exploitation
> - The extent to which appropriate open science practices are implemented as an integral part of the
>   proposed project to ensure open sharing and wide distribution of research outputs.
> - Quality and scope of communication and engagement activities with different target audiences, including
>   relevant stakeholders/users.

**Important nuance**: societal impact is *optional* — FRIPRO does not require it, and reviewers only score
it if the applicant raises it. Don't penalize a purely basic-science proposal for lacking societal framing;
do check that if societal impact is claimed, it's plausible and not overreached (overclaiming societal
relevance for basic research is a credibility risk with reviewers, not a strength).

Weaknesses to look for:

- Academic impact described only as "we will publish in top journals" without saying what specifically
  becomes newly possible for others in the field (new dataset, new method, new theoretical framework
  others can build on).
- No concrete open science / open access / data-sharing plan, or one that's generic rather than tied to
  what this project will actually produce (which datasets, code, protocols will be shared, and how/where).
- Dissemination plan that's just a list of conferences, with no discussion of engagement beyond the
  immediate academic community if stakeholders are relevant to the topic.
- Overreaching societal-impact claims not supported by the project's actual scope — this reads as
  unconvincing to specialist reviewers and can drag down the "plausibility" sub-criterion.

## 4. Implementation

> The quality of the project manager and project group
> - The extent to which the project manager has relevant expertise and experience, and demonstrated ability
>   to perform high-quality research (as appropriate to the career stage).
> - The degree of complementarity of the participants and the extent to which the project group has the
>   necessary expertise needed to undertake the research effectively.
>
> The quality of the project organisation and management
> - Effectiveness of the project organisation, including the extent to which resources assigned to work
>   packages are aligned with project objectives and deliverables.
> - Appropriateness of the allocation of tasks, ensuring that all participants have a valid role and
>   adequate resources in the project to fulfil that role.
> - Appropriateness of the proposed management structures and governance.

**What reviewers are really asking**: can this specific team, with this specific plan, actually deliver
what's promised, on this budget and timeline?

Weaknesses to look for:

- PM's track record described in generic terms (publication counts, grants held) rather than demonstrating
  specific prior capability directly relevant to *this* project's riskiest components.
- Team assembled without a clear account of complementary expertise — e.g. missing a named collaborator or
  skill set for a technique central to the plan, or team members listed without a clear role.
- Work packages that don't map cleanly to the budget (see `fripro-call-details.md` on budget) — check that
  headcount/effort and money requested are consistent with the described work.
- Timeline/Gantt chart that's superficial or missing, given a 3–8 year project — reviewers want to see
  realistic milestones and sequencing, including dependencies and go/no-go decision points tied to the
  risk-management plan from Criterion 2.
- Governance for multi-partner or interdisciplinary projects not addressed (who decides what, how conflicts
  are resolved) — more important the more partners/disciplines are involved.

What to recommend: an explicit work-package table (objective → activities → deliverable → responsible
person → resources) if the draft doesn't already have one, since it lets a reviewer check consistency
across the whole document in one place.
